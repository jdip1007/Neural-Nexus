    Australian tax residence guide

    https://www.bentleys.com.au/insights/understanding-tax-residency-status-for-tax-purposes-in-australia/ 19/6/2026, 12:29 Page 1 of 12 Understanding tax residency status for Tax Purposes in Australia May 29, 2026 Your tax residency status is one of the most consequential determinations the ATO will make about you. Get it right and you know exactly what you owe. Get it wrong and you could face unexpected bills, penalties, or miss out on offsets you were entitled to claim. The core issue is straightforward: Australian tax residents pay tax on their worldwide income. Non-residents pay tax only on income sourced within Australia. That single distinction can mean thousands of dollars of difference in your annual tax position, especially if you have overseas income, assets held abroad, or a period of working internationally. The Australian Taxation Office ( (https://www.ato.gov.au/) ATO ) uses a series of statutory tests to determine where you sit. Understanding those tests, and how they apply to your personal circumstances, is the starting point for getting this right. What Is the Importance of Tax Residency Status? Tax residency status determines the full scope of your Australian tax obligations. Residents are assessed on all income, regardless of where in the world it was earned. That includes salary from a foreign employer, rental income from an overseas property, and returns from international investments. Non-residents face a narrower obligation but are generally taxed at higher rates on their Australian-sourced income, and they lose access to the tax-free threshold. So while a non- resident might owe no Australian tax on foreign income, they will pay more on whatever they do earn here. Residency status also affects your eligibility for the Medicare levy, the foreign income tax offset, and certain capital gains tax concessions. For anyone moving to Australia, leaving the country, or splitting time between two jurisdictions, getting the right classification early avoids compounding problems at lodgement time. Key Tests for Determining Your Tax Residency Status The ATO does not rely on a single test to classify your residency. It applies to up to four statutory tests in sequence. If you satisfy any one of them, you are an Australian tax resident for that financial year. The tests are the Resides Test (the primary test), the Domicile Test, the 183-Day Test, and the Commonwealth Superannuation Test. Each looks at different aspects of your connection to Australia, from your lifestyle and living arrangements to your physical presence and employment status. They work independently. Failing one does not prevent you from satisfying another. The Resides Test The Resides Test is the starting point and asks a basic question: does this person live in Australia? The answer requires more than counting nights spent here. The ATO looks at the totality of your circumstances to decide whether Australia is genuinely where you reside. Factors considered include where your family lives, where you are employed, whether you own or rent property in Australia, your participation in community or social activities, and how regularly you return here. No single factor is determinative. Someone who splits their time between Australia and another country might still pass the Resides Test if the weight of their personal and economic ties is firmly here. If the Resides Test is satisfied, you are a resident. If it is not, the ATO moves to the remaining tests. The Domicile Test for Tax The Domicile Test applies where someone does not appear to reside in Australia in the ordinary sense but may still have Australia as their permanent home. It is particularly relevant for Australians living or working overseas temporarily, such as those on an expatriate assignment or posted abroad for work. https://www.bentleys.com.au/insights/understanding-tax-residency-status-for-tax-purposes-in-australia/ 19/6/2026, 12:29 Page 3 of 12 Under the Domicile Test, you are an Australian tax resident if your domicile (your permanent home) is in Australia, unless the ATO is satisfied that your permanent place of abode is outside Australia. That last qualification matters. If you have moved overseas and established a genuine long-term home there, the Domicile Test may not catch you, even if you were born in Australia or hold an Australian passport. Property ownership, family ties, where your assets are held, and the duration and intent of your overseas stay all feed into this assessment. The 183-Day Rule Australia The 183-Day Test provides a more mechanical trigger. Spend 183 days or more in Australia during a financial year and you will generally be classified as a tax resident for that year, even without any intention of making Australia your permanent home. The count runs across the full financial year (1 July to 30 June). Days of arrival and departure are typically counted as full days. The test can catch people who have been in Australia on an extended work project, even without any plan to stay permanently. There is an exception. If you can demonstrate that your usual place of abode is outside Australia and you had no intention of taking up residence here, the 183-day count alone may not be enough to classify you as a resident. But this exception is applied narrowly. If you are spending that much time in Australia, the ATO will examine your circumstances closely. The Permanent Place of Abode The Permanent Place of Abode test runs alongside the Domicile Test rather than operating as a standalone measure. It is the mechanism that prevents someone from being treated as an Australian tax resident purely because their domicile is here, where they have genuinely established a long-term home overseas. To satisfy this exemption, your overseas home must be more than temporary accommodation. A short-term rental while on contract work abroad would generally not qualify. A home you have bought, furnished, and settled into with your family, with no immediate intention to return to Australia, is more likely to meet the standard. Factors the ATO considers include the duration of your overseas stay, whether you have sold or rented out your Australian property, where your spouse and children are living, and whether you maintain social and financial ties within Australia. https://www.bentleys.com.au/insights/understanding-tax-residency-status-for-tax-purposes-in-australia/ 19/6/2026, 12:29 Page 4 of 12 The Commonwealth Superannuation Test The Commonwealth Superannuation Test is a narrow test that applies only to Australian government employees posted overseas. If you are a member of the Public Sector Superannuation Scheme, the Commonwealth Superannuation Scheme, or a similar government fund, and you are working abroad, you may be classified as an Australian tax resident under this test regardless of your physical location. The policy reasoning is sensible. Government employees working overseas are there at the direction of the Australian Government, not because they have chosen to relocate. Treating them as non-residents would create inconsistencies relative to colleagues working within Australia. Government employees and contractors should take advice on how this test interacts with their broader tax position, including superannuation planning. Bentleys’ (https://www.bentleys.com.au/services/tax-advisory/) tax advisors work with clients in both public and private sectors on these cross-border arrangements. Special Cases and Exemptions Working Holiday Makers Working holiday makers, those who hold a subclass 417 or 462 visa, are taxed at a flat rate on their Australian income rather than the progressive rates applied to residents. From the 2017 financial year onwards, that rate has been 15% on the first $45,000 of taxable income, with ordinary non-resident rates applying above that threshold. Working holiday makers are generally treated as non-residents for tax purposes, regardless of how many days they spend in Australia, and they do not have access to the tax-free threshold. Temporary Residents Temporary residents enjoy a partial exemption from Australian tax. If you hold a temporary visa and are not the spouse of an Australian citizen or permanent resident, you are generally taxed only on your Australian-sourced income and employment income attributable to your time here. Most foreign income, including overseas interest, dividends, and capital gains from foreign assets, is excluded from your assessable Australian income. The temporary resident regime is a deliberate concession, recognising that people here on a provisional basis have not fully committed to the country and should not carry the same tax obligations as someone who has settled permanently. https://www.bentleys.com.au/insights/understanding-tax-residency-status-for-tax-purposes-in-australia/ 19/6/2026, 12:29 Page 5 of 12 Part-Year Residency If your residency status changes during a financial year, different rules apply to each segment of the year. When you become an Australian tax resident, you are assessed as a resident from that date. When you cease to be a resident, non-resident treatment applies from that point. The ATO has specific guidance on how to handle income allocation and asset disposal timing in these situations, and getting the timing right can have a meaningful effect on your tax bill. The Impact of Tax Residency on Your Taxes The headline impact is straightforward. Residents pay tax on global income at progressive Australian rates, the rate for 2025-26 is 16 cents in the dollar on income from $18,201 to $45,000, following the Stage 3 tax cuts that took effect from 1 July 2024. Non-residents pay no Australian tax on foreign income but face higher flat rates on Australian income, with no tax- free threshold applying. The Medicare levy (currently 2% of taxable income) applies to residents but not non-residents. Higher-income residents without private hospital cover may also face the Medicare levy surcharge. For capital gains tax purposes, non-residents are only subject to CGT on “taxable Australian property”, primarily Australian real estate and interests in entities with predominantly real property assets. The 50% CGT discount that residents can access after holding an asset for 12 months is generally not available to foreign residents. These differences add up quickly. For someone with significant global income or a large property portfolio, the resident/non-resident distinction could mean tens of thousands of dollars per year. It is also worth noting that withholding tax obligations can arise when Australian entities make payments such as interest, dividends, or royalties to foreign residents. Bentleys’ (https://www.bentleys.com.au/client-alert-tax-news-september-2024/) September 2024 client alert on withholding for foreign residents explains these obligations in more detail, including when exemptions might apply. Double Taxation Agreements and Foreign Income Tax Offset Australia has signed double taxation agreements (DTAs) with over 40 countries. These agreements allocate taxing rights over specific income types so that the same income is not taxed in full by two countries simultaneously. DTAs commonly cover salary, dividends, royalties, pensions, and capital gains, specifying which country has the primary right to tax each type and at what rate. https://www.bentleys.com.au/insights/understanding-tax-residency-status-for-tax-purposes-in-australia/ 19/6/2026, 12:29 Page 6 of 12 If you are an Australian resident who has already paid tax overseas on foreign income, you may be able to claim the foreign income tax offset (FITO) to reduce your Australian tax liability. The offset is generally capped at the lesser of the foreign tax paid and the Australian tax that would otherwise be payable on that income. It removes double taxation without resulting in a refund exceeding what you paid abroad. DTAs can also assist with residency determination itself. Some agreements include tie-breaker rules that determine which country a person is a tax resident of when both countries would otherwise claim them. If you are living or working in a DTA country and your residency status is uncertain, the relevant agreement is worth examining carefully. The (https://www.ato.gov.au/individuals-and-families/coming-to-australia-or-going-overseas/your-tax-residency) ATO’s guidance on tax residency for individuals includes information on how DTAs interact with the Australian tests. Tax Residency Self-Assessment Australia operates on a self-assessment basis. You determine your own tax residency status, and you are responsible for the accuracy of that determination. The ATO does not pre-approve your classification before you lodge your return. That creates a genuine record-keeping obligation. If you are claiming non-residency, you should be able to document your time spent outside Australia, your overseas living arrangements, and the nature of your ties to other countries. Travel records, lease agreements, employment contracts, and bank statements are all potentially relevant. The ATO does audit residency claims and tends to scrutinise arrangements that look contrived. A common mistake is assuming that simply being outside Australia for a period makes you a non-resident, particularly where you have maintained a home here, kept your family in Australia, or continued to earn Australian income. If your circumstances are anything other than clear-cut, seeking advice before lodgement is a better investment than addressing an ATO amendment after the fact. Residency Status Impact on Tax Rates For most people, the difference in tax rates between residents and non-residents is material. Residents have access to offsets, deductions, and concessions, including the low income tax offset and the seniors and pensioners tax offset, that non-residents cannot claim. Residents also benefit from the tax-free threshold and access to the CGT discount on eligible assets. https://www.bentleys.com.au/insights/understanding-tax-residency-status-for-tax-purposes-in-australia/ 19/6/2026, 12:29 Page 7 of 12 For those in a borderline situation, getting the classification right matters in both directions. Claiming non-resident status when you are actually a resident creates a risk of penalties and interest if the ATO disagrees. Claiming resident status when you are actually a non-resident could mean overpaying tax unnecessarily. Bentleys’ (https://www.bentleys.com.au/insights/international-tax-guide-for-foreign-companies-doing-business-in- australia/) guide to international tax for individuals and foreign companies explores how Australian tax rates and obligations apply across different residency classifications and business structures. Where to Go From Here Tax residency is not a set-and-forget classification. Your status can change when you leave Australia, return from overseas, change employment, sell property, or alter your living arrangements significantly. Reviewing your position whenever there is a material change in your circumstances keeps you ahead of any problems. If you are confident in your classification, keep documentation that would support it through an audit. If there is any uncertainty, professional advice before lodgement is cheaper and less stressful than dealing with an ATO review after the fact. The (https://www.ato.gov.au/individuals-and-families/coming-to-australia-or-going-overseas/your-tax-residency) ATO’s residency determination too l is a useful starting point for straightforward cases. For anything involving multiple countries, foreign assets, or recent changes in your living arrangements, a conversation with a specialist tax adviser will give you considerably more certainty. FAQs Am I an Australian resident for tax purposes? https://www.bentleys.com.au/insights/understanding-tax-residency-status-for-tax-purposes-in-australia/ 19/6/2026, 12:29 Page 8 of 12 You are generally considered an Australian resident for tax purposes if you satisfy at least one of the ATO’s residency tests, including the resides test, domicile test, 183-day test, or Commonwealth superannuation test. What is the difference between tax residency and immigration status? Tax residency is separate from immigration status. You can be a temporary visa holder but still be an Australian tax resident, or hold permanent residency while remaining a foreign resident for tax purposes. How does the resides test work in Australia? The resides test examines whether you live in Australia according to ordinary concepts, considering factors such as family ties, lifestyle, work arrangements, and where your assets are located. What is the 183-day test for Australian tax? If you spend 183 days or more in Australia during a financial year, you may be considered a tax resident unless your usual place of abode is overseas and you do not intend to reside in Australia. Do I need to pay tax on my worldwide income as an Australian resident? Yes, Australian tax residents generally must declare all worldwide income, including overseas salary, investments, and foreign rental income. What are the tax rates for foreign residents in Australia? For the 2025–26 financial year, foreign residents are taxed at 30 per cent up to $135,000, 37 per cent between $135,001 and $190,000, and 45 per cent above $190,000. Is there a tax-free threshold for Australian tax residents? Yes, Australian residents receive the standard tax-free threshold of $18,200, though this may be adjusted for part-year residency. What is the domicile test for Australian residency? You may be considered a resident if your legal domicile is in Australia unless you establish a permanent place of abode outside Australia. https://www.bentleys.com.au/insights/understanding-tax-residency-status-for-tax-purposes-in-australia/ 19/6/2026, 12:29 Page 9 of 12 What constitutes a permanent place of abode outside Australia? The ATO generally looks for a settled, long-term overseas living arrangement with continuity and permanence rather than temporary accommodation. How does being a temporary resident affect my Australian tax? Temporary residents are generally only taxed on Australian-sourced income and are exempt from tax on most foreign income. Do working holiday makers count as Australian residents? Most working holiday makers are taxed under special rates starting at 15 per cent on the first $45,000 of Australian income regardless of residency status. How is the financial year defined for Australian tax residency? The Australian financial year runs from 1 July to 30 June, and residency tests are applied across this period. Does owning property in Australia make me a tax resident? Owning property alone does not determine residency, though it is one factor the ATO considers when assessing your overall circumstances. What is the Commonwealth superannuation test? This test applies to certain Australian Government employees and members of eligible Commonwealth superannuation schemes working overseas. Can I be a resident of two countries for tax purposes? Yes, dual residency is possible. In these cases, double taxation agreements usually contain tie- breaker rules to determine which country has primary taxing rights. Do foreign residents pay the Medicare levy? Foreign residents are generally exempt from the Medicare levy if they are not entitled to Medicare benefits. What happens if my residency status changes mid-year? https://www.bentleys.com.au/insights/understanding-tax-residency-status-for-tax-purposes-in-australia/ 19/6/2026, 12:29 Page 10 of 12 You may become a part-year resident, meaning different tax rules apply to different portions of the financial year. Is a holiday house in Australia considered a permanent place of abode? Generally not. Holiday homes usually lack the permanence and continuity required to establish residency for tax purposes. How does the ATO view short-term work contracts for residency? Short-term contracts alone do not automatically create residency, especially if you maintain stronger personal and financial ties overseas. Does the 183-day test apply to a calendar year? No, the test applies specifically to the Australian financial year from 1 July to 30 June. Will my overseas student debt affect my residency status? No, HELP or student debt does not determine residency, though Australian residents living overseas must still report worldwide income for repayment purposes. Do I need to inform the ATO when I leave Australia permanently? Yes, you should notify the ATO when your residency status changes, often through your final Australian tax return. How is Capital Gains Tax (CGT) applied to foreign residents? Foreign residents are generally only taxed on gains relating to taxable Australian property, such as Australian real estate. Can I use the ATO residency tool for a definitive answer? The ATO residency tool provides guidance only. Complex cases often require advice from a qualified Australian tax professional. Not automatically. You may still argue that your usual place of abode remains overseas and that you had no intention of residing permanently in Australia. Are temporary residents taxed on their foreign income? Generally, no. Most foreign investment income earned by temporary residents is excluded from Australian assessable income. What happens if my residency status changes partway through the year? A split-year treatment applies, meaning different tax rules and thresholds apply to resident and non-resident periods separately.
