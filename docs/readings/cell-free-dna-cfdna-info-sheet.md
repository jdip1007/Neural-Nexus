## Detailed Notes

### cfDNA Origins and Characteristics

Cell-free DNA consists of fragmented DNA molecules circulating in various body fluids:

- **Physiological Sources**: Normal cell turnover, apoptosis, necrosis
- **Pathological Sources**: Tumor cell death, inflammation, tissue damage
- **Fragment Size**: Typically 150-200 base pairs nucleosomal fragments
- **Concentration**: Varies by health status and disease condition
- **Half-life**: Short circulation time requiring rapid processing

### Clinical Applications

#### Cancer Diagnostics
- **Early Detection**: Identification of tumor-specific mutations
- **Monitoring**: Treatment response and disease progression
- **Minimal Residual Disease**: Detection of remaining cancer cells
- **Tumor Heterogeneity**: Assessment of genetic diversity

#### Non-Cancer Applications
- **Prenatal Testing**: Fetal DNA in maternal circulation
- **Organ Transplant**: Graft rejection monitoring
- **Inflammatory Diseases**: Disease activity assessment
- **Trauma Assessment**: Tissue damage evaluation

### Extraction Methods

cfDNA extraction requires specialized approaches due to low concentration:

- **Blood Collection**: Specialized tubes preventing DNA degradation
- **Plasma Separation**: Double centrifugation for cell-free fraction
- **Enrichment Strategies**: Size selection, carrier RNA addition
- **Purification**: Optimized kits for low-concentration DNA
- **Quality Control**: Specific metrics for cfDNA assessment

### Diagnostic Utility

cfDNA enables revolutionary diagnostic approaches:

- **Non-Invasive Testing**: Eliminates need for tissue biopsies
- **Longitudinal Monitoring**: Repeated sampling over time
- **Early Detection**: Identifies disease before clinical symptoms
- **Personalized Medicine**: Tailored treatment based on molecular profile
- **Cost-Effectiveness**: Reduces invasive procedures and hospitalizations
